# 11 — Copy Specification

This file tells Claude Code how to write. The data spec (`01`) tells it what numbers to use. The template spec (`04`) tells it where to put sections. This file tells it the voice, tone, sentence-level patterns, and industry-specific psychological framing that determines whether a business owner reads the page or bounces.

The copy must serve two audiences simultaneously: the human business owner who is making one of the largest financial decisions of their life, and Google's content quality systems that assess whether a page demonstrates genuine expertise or templated fluff. These two audiences are not in conflict — they reward the same things. A page that genuinely serves the owner will perform better in both dimensions than a page optimized for either audience alone.

---

## The Person Reading This Page

Before writing a single word, establish which of these three people is most likely arriving at this specific industry page:

**The owner planning ahead (2-5 year horizon).** Built the business over many years. Emotionally attached. Skeptical of online tools. Worried about being lowballed. Primary emotional state: cautious hope mixed with some anxiety. What they want: honest information that helps them understand their realistic position before they engage with a broker. They will read carefully if the content feels like it was written by someone who understands their industry.

**The owner who just received an offer.** Someone called them out of the blue — a PE firm, a competitor, a broker who heard they might be interested. They are now doing urgent research to understand if the offer is reasonable. Primary emotional state: urgent, slightly suspicious, time-pressured. What they want: a fast answer to "is this number realistic or is someone trying to take advantage of me?" They need specific numbers quickly. They will scroll past generic content without slowing down.

**The owner triggered by an external event.** A peer just sold for a surprising number. A health issue made exit planning suddenly real. A divorce or partnership dispute requires a valuation. Primary emotional state: newly motivated, sometimes emotionally activated. What they want: to understand what their options look like. Language that opens a door rather than closes it.

The hook sentence on each industry page must address one of these three states — implicitly, not by announcing which state you're addressing. The right hook makes the reader feel recognized before they consciously register why.

## The Three-Element Opening Structure

Every industry page opens with three elements in this exact order. These three elements together span approximately 100-130 words before the calculator.

### Element 1: The Hook Sentence

One sentence. Maximum two. Written for the human. Reflects the specific emotional context of selling a business in this industry. References a current market reality or common seller misconception that the data resolves.

**What it is not:** "Are you thinking about selling your [industry] business?" This is the most common opening on competitor pages and it is precisely what this site must not sound like.

**What it is:** A sentence that demonstrates immediate familiarity with the seller's specific situation — something only someone who has studied this industry's sale dynamics would say.

**Pattern:** [Specific market reality or common assumption about this industry] + [what the data actually shows or what the real question is].

**Examples by industry:**

*HVAC:* "The PE roll-up wave that's been acquiring HVAC companies since 2022 has permanently changed what buyers will pay — even for single-location owner-operated businesses without a service contract book."

*Restaurant:* "Most restaurant owners expect a number somewhere between 2 and 3 times earnings; what the BizBuySell data actually shows is that lease terms and liquor license status matter as much as the earnings figure itself."

*Car Wash:* "Car wash owners are often shocked to learn their business trades at nearly 5 times annual earnings — one of the highest multiples in the entire Main Street market — and the reason is almost entirely about whether the business has a membership program."

*Funeral Home:* "Funeral home owners often underestimate what their business is worth because they think of it as a service business; buyers and consolidators see it as an essential-service franchise with a captive market and demographics that only improve with time."

*Laundromat:* "Most laundromat owners think they're running a 2-3x business; the transaction data shows the opposite — modern card-payment laundromats with functioning equipment consistently trade above 3.5x, and some approach 5x."

*Accounting Practice:* "Accounting practice buyers don't pay for the work — they pay for the client relationships, which means a practice where the clients know and trust only the selling partner is worth less than one where they've developed relationships across the team."

*Dental Practice:* "The DSO acquisition wave has changed dental practice valuation in two directions simultaneously: it has raised what the best practices can get, and it has made it easier for practice owners to confuse a DSO's headline offer with what the business is actually worth."

*Nail Salon:* "Nail salons trade at some of the lower multiples in the personal care category — not because the businesses aren't profitable, but because client loyalty tends to follow the technician rather than the address, which buyers price as transition risk."

Each hook is specific, demonstrates industry knowledge, and addresses something the target seller either already suspects, has heard from a peer, or is actively wondering about. None of them are generic.

---

### Element 2: The Direct Answer Block

This is the 2-3 sentence block that answers the core query directly. It is structurally distinct from the hook — visually set apart (can be styled as a summary card or a callout box in the design). It is the primary SGE extraction target: the fragment most likely to be cited by Google's AI Overview systems.

**What it must contain:**
- The specific SDE multiple range for this industry (low × to high ×)
- The median or mid multiple
- The data source and transaction count
- A one-sentence statement of what the range depends on

**Exact pattern:**
"[Industry] businesses typically sell for [sde_multiple_low]× to [sde_multiple_high]× annual SDE, with a median around [sde_multiple_mid]×, based on [transaction_count]+ closed transactions reported to BizBuySell through Q4 2025. Where a specific business falls within that range depends primarily on [1-2 specific factors most important for this industry]. Use the calculator below to see how your specific numbers and situation affect the estimate."

**HVAC example:**
"HVAC businesses typically sell for 2.3× to 3.5× annual SDE, with a median around 2.79×, based on 420+ closed transactions reported to BizBuySell through Q4 2025. Where a specific business falls in that range depends most heavily on recurring service contract revenue as a percentage of total revenue, and how dependent daily operations are on the owner's direct involvement. Use the calculator below to see how your specific numbers and situation affect the estimate."

**Why this structure:**
The numbers answer the query. The "depends on" sentence creates the reason to use the calculator rather than taking the number and leaving. The final sentence is a direct bridge to the tool. All three purposes are served in 60-70 words.

This block does not repeat the hook sentence's market context. It does not editorialize. It answers. Direct Answer Blocks that contain opinion or context beyond what was asked are less likely to be extracted by AI systems because they introduce ambiguity into what is a factual answer.

### Element 3: The Orienting Paragraph

One short paragraph (40-60 words) that tells the owner what the rest of the page contains and why it is worth reading past the calculator result. This is not a table of contents. It is a value statement for the content below.

**Pattern:** "Below the calculator, you'll find [specific useful thing 1] and [specific useful thing 2] — the factors that move [industry] valuations up or down, who actually buys these businesses and how they finance it, and [specific useful thing 3 relevant to this industry]."

**HVAC example:**
"Below the calculator, you'll find the specific factors that move HVAC valuations up or down — including how service contract revenue is calculated by buyers, why PE consolidators pay different multiples than individual buyers, and what to do if your business is heavily dependent on your personal involvement in field operations."

This paragraph keeps scrollers reading. It is also a topical authority signal — it names specific, relevant concepts that a genuine HVAC industry expert would identify as the real questions.

## Voice and Tone Standards

**Direct, not clinical.** The content should read like a knowledgeable person explaining something clearly, not like a report. Sentences should be complete and clear. Avoid passive voice except where it reads awkwardly in active.

**Honest about bad news.** Do not soften risk factors. Do not bury them at the end. Do not use euphemisms ("challenges" instead of "buyers will discount for this"). A seller who reads that their owner-dependent business will trade at the lower multiple has received genuinely useful information — and they will trust everything else on the page more as a result.

**Specific, not general.** Generic statements kill trust instantly. "Recurring revenue is valuable" is generic. "HVAC maintenance agreements representing 30%+ of annual revenue can add 0.5× to 0.8× to your sale multiple because buyers treat this revenue as near-guaranteed" is specific. Every claim should have a number, a mechanism, or an industry-specific example attached to it.

**Treats the owner as competent.** These are people who have successfully run businesses for 10-30 years. They are capable of handling real information. Condescension — including the soft condescension of over-explaining basic concepts — will trigger disengagement. The SDE explainer in the calculator is written for people who don't know the term; the supporting content below is written for people who do.

**Does not oversell the tool.** The tool is a free estimation resource. It is not a formal appraisal. The content should acknowledge this clearly — not because it's required legally, but because an honest statement of limitations builds more trust than a claim that oversells accuracy. "This gives you a data-backed planning estimate — not the number a certified appraiser or broker would produce after reviewing your financials, but an honest starting point based on what similar businesses have actually sold for."

## The "What Increases Value" Section

**Section heading pattern:** "What Makes a [Industry] Business Worth More"

**Not a bullet list.** This section in the page copy is prose with embedded bullets or short paragraphs — not a flat list. Each factor gets expansion. The JSON's `what_increases_value` array contains the seed phrases; the page copy expands each into 2-3 sentences explaining the mechanism, not just naming the factor.

**The expansion pattern for each factor:**
1. State the factor in one clear phrase
2. Explain why buyers specifically value it (the mechanism)
3. Quantify if possible (how much it affects the multiple, or what threshold matters)

**HVAC maintenance agreement example:**

*JSON seed:* "Maintenance agreement base covering 30%+ of annual revenue"

*Page copy expansion:*
"A maintenance agreement base representing 30% or more of annual revenue is the single most reliable way to move toward the upper-quartile multiple in an HVAC sale. Buyers — particularly the PE-backed platforms that have been acquiring HVAC businesses since 2022 — treat recurring maintenance revenue almost like a subscription: it is pre-sold, it recurs without re-acquisition cost, and it creates touchpoints for the equipment replacement sales that generate the highest margins. A business with $500,000 SDE and 40% maintenance contract revenue will be priced differently than an identical business dependent on new installation revenue, even with identical earnings numbers."

**Restaurant lease example:**

*JSON seed:* "Long-term, transferable lease with below-market rent"

*Page copy expansion:*
"A lease with 7+ years remaining at below-market rent is often worth more to the sale price than an equivalent improvement in earnings. Here's why: SBA lenders — who finance the majority of restaurant acquisitions — require the loan term to be covered by the lease. A restaurant with a lease expiring in 18 months may be impossible to finance at any price. A restaurant with 10 years of below-market rent has a competitive cost structure that a buyer can model with confidence through the loan repayment period."

## The "What Reduces Value" Section

**Section heading pattern:** "What Can Lower a [Industry] Business's Sale Price"

This is the most important section for building trust with the owner who is in the "received an unsolicited offer" state. They already suspect there is something about their business that might be a problem. Naming it clearly — before they sit down with a buyer who will use it as a negotiating lever — is what separates a genuinely useful resource from a marketing piece.

**The tone here is direct, not alarming.** The goal is not to discourage sellers but to give them information they can act on. Where the risk factor is correctable before sale, say so. Where it is not, say so equally clearly.

**The expansion pattern:**

1. Name the specific risk factor — do not soften it
2. Explain the mechanism: why buyers discount for this specifically
3. If correctable before sale: state what the correction would require
4. If not correctable: name what the realistic expectation is

**HVAC owner-dependency example:**

*JSON seed:* "Owner is the primary or only certified technician"

*Page copy expansion:*
"The single most common reason HVAC businesses trade at the lower end of the range — or fail to close entirely — is an owner who is also the primary technician. Buyers do not discount arbitrarily for this: they are pricing the real cost of replacing a skilled HVAC technician ($70,000-$90,000 in annual salary plus benefits, plus a search and onboarding period where service quality dips). If you are the lead technician in your business, the path to a higher multiple runs through either hiring and training a qualified replacement technician before listing, or pricing the business with that replacement cost built into the negotiation. There is no shortcut. A buyer who discovers this during diligence — rather than at initial presentation — will discount more aggressively than one who was told upfront."

**Restaurant short lease example:**

*JSON seed:* "Short lease with no renewal option or uncertain renewal terms"

*Page copy expansion:*
"A lease expiring within 18-24 months is the most common deal-killer in restaurant M&A — not because buyers are unreasonable, but because the bank financing the acquisition requires the loan to be secured by a lease that covers the loan term. A restaurant with a strong earnings history but a 12-month lease remaining will struggle to find a financeable buyer at any price. If your lease is approaching renewal, the time to negotiate an extension is before you engage a broker — not during the sale process, when the landlord knows you need the deal."

## FAQ Section

**Heading pattern:** "Frequently Asked Questions: Selling a [Industry] Business"

**Question format:** Natural human language. Not "What is the SDE multiple for HVAC businesses?" but "How much is an HVAC business worth?" Not every question needs to contain a keyword. Questions that read naturally will be used naturally by AI systems that are increasingly trained to match conversational intent.

**Answer format:**
- 3-5 complete sentences
- First sentence answers the question directly — does not begin with "It depends" or a restatement of the question
- Include at least one specific number or data point
- For questions about current value or market conditions, include a temporal signal — reference current interest rates, current SBA loan availability, or a 2024-2026 market condition specific to this industry. This is what differentiates a 2026 answer from a 2022 answer on the same topic and signals freshness to both readers and AI extraction systems
- End with either an action (use the calculator, read this guide) or a qualifier that prevents misapplication

**Prohibited FAQ patterns:**
- "This is a complex question with many factors..." — this answers nothing
- Ending an answer with "consult a professional" without first actually answering — the disclaimer can come at the end after a real answer
- Repeating the question in the answer: "The SDE multiple for HVAC businesses is..." as an opener — just answer it
- Generic questions that could appear on any valuation page: "What is SDE?" belongs in the calculator inline help, not in an industry-specific FAQ

**Required: at least one FAQ question that could only be asked by someone selling this specific type of business.** For HVAC: "Does having a service contract book make a meaningful difference in what my business sells for?" For restaurants: "Will a liquor license increase what my restaurant is worth?" For dental: "How does a DSO acquisition offer compare to selling to another dentist?" These are the questions that signal genuine industry depth.

**FAQ example — HVAC:**

Q: How much is an HVAC business worth?
A: Based on BizBuySell data from 420+ closed transactions, HVAC businesses typically sell for 2.3× to 3.5× annual SDE, with a median around 2.79×. A business generating $200,000 in annual SDE would be valued between $460,000 and $700,000 under typical market conditions — though the actual range shifts significantly based on service contract revenue, owner dependency, and whether the buyer is an individual operator or a PE-backed consolidator. Use the calculator above to see a range specific to your numbers and situation.

Q: Does my HVAC service contract book affect what I can sell for?
A: Yes — significantly. Buyers, particularly the PE-backed consolidators that have been acquiring HVAC companies aggressively since 2022, treat recurring maintenance agreement revenue almost like SaaS subscriptions: predictable, low-churn, and requiring no reselling cost. A business where 40%+ of revenue comes from maintenance agreements can realistically support a multiple at the upper end of the range. A business dependent primarily on new installation and replacement will trade lower, because that revenue requires continuous marketing and is subject to seasonality and economic cycles.

Q: What's the difference between what a private equity buyer will pay versus an individual owner-operator?
A: PE-backed consolidators and individual buyers are effectively two different markets. Individual owner-operators acquiring a business to operate it themselves typically apply the SDE multiples you see in the BizBuySell data — 2.3×-3.5× for HVAC. PE platforms paying strategic acquisition premiums for businesses with scale, strong maintenance contract books, and professional management can pay significantly more. If you have received an unsolicited offer from a PE-backed platform, compare it against the SDE-multiple range as a sanity check — but recognize that their offer may reflect strategic value that a standard multiple calculation won't capture.

## The Contextual Internal Link Pattern

Internal links within body copy must not appear as standalone "click here" sentences. They must be embedded as natural clauses in sentences that are already serving the reader.

**Wrong pattern:** "Learn more about SDE by reading our SDE explainer guide."

**Right pattern:** "If you are not certain how to calculate your SDE — which adds your salary, owner benefits, and non-recurring expenses back to net profit — [our SDE calculation guide walks through the exact add-backs with examples for service businesses like HVAC]."

The anchor text is the bracketed phrase — descriptive, specific, and a natural continuation of the sentence. The reader understands what they will get before clicking. The surrounding context signals to Google what the destination page covers.

**The anchor text formula:** [Contextual Statement] + [Descriptive Anchor] + [Expected Value]. Every internal link in body copy should follow this structure — a sentence that provides semantic context before the anchor, an anchor that describes the destination specifically, and a phrase after the anchor that states what the reader will get by clicking. This three-part structure gives both the reader and Google's link analysis system the full context of what the link connects.

**Wrong pattern:** "Use our [HVAC valuation tool] today."

**Right pattern:** "To see how your recurring service contract revenue specifically affects your exit price, use the [HVAC business valuation calculator] to get a data-backed range based on 420+ closed transactions."

The surrounding semantic neighborhood — "recurring service contract revenue," "exit price," "data-backed range," "closed transactions" — is what Google's 2026 link analysis uses to verify the link's relevance to both the source page and the destination. A link with no semantic neighborhood is a weaker signal than the same link embedded in relevant context.

**Internal links to guide pages** must be placed where the guide page's content is most relevant to the specific thing the owner is currently reading about — not in a "Related Reading" block at the bottom. Links that appear in context, at the moment of maximum relevance, generate more clicks and stronger topical relevance signals.

## What Claude Code Must Not Write

These patterns will appear if Claude Code defaults to generic content generation. They are explicitly prohibited:

**Filler openers:** "In today's competitive business landscape..." / "Whether you're a small business owner or an entrepreneur..." / "Selling a business is a complex process..." — none of these. Every page opens with the industry-specific hook.

**The hedge paragraph:** A paragraph explaining that valuation is complex, depends on many factors, and that every business is different. This is true but it communicates nothing useful and signals to both readers and Google that the content has no real information density.

**Advice to "consult a professional" as the primary answer:** Disclaimers belong at the end of sections, after the actual content has been delivered. Opening with or centering on "you should speak to a certified appraiser" without first delivering real information is content avoidance. The site exists to give owners a genuine data-backed starting point — that mission must be executed before any disclaimers.

**Keyword stuffing in headings:** "Best HVAC Business Valuation Calculator Tool for Small Business Owners 2026" is not a heading. It is a keyword list. Headings should describe what the section contains in the most direct language possible.

**Passive voice for negative information:** "Lower multiples may be seen in situations where owner involvement is high" should be "Businesses where the owner is the primary technician trade at the lower end — buyers are pricing the cost of replacing that labor." Active, direct, specific.

**False precision:** "Your HVAC business is worth $487,392." The calculator output is always a range, rounded to the nearest $5,000. The copy surrounding it should reinforce that this is a planning estimate, not an appraisal.
